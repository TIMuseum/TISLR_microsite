Reimagining Place

Geological Past, Present and Future on the Islands

The 400-acre Treasure Island (TI) was fabricated in the 1930s on top of different densities of San Francisco Bay mud and sand. Since construction, a northwestern section of the island has been sinking along with its particularly unstable and soft, sand and mud foundation.

Treasure Island was constructed in the 1930s at the same time as what was then known as the "new" and first Bay Bridge. (Treasure Island Museum, 1937)

At the same time sea level has continued to rise, and now it is increasing at an accelerated pace. Geologists believe that without the adaptations planned to address sea level rise (SLR), this northwest stretch of Treasure Island would sink at the same rate at which the Bay is rising.

Yerba Buena Island is in the background as Treasure Island is built upon the shoals. The sandy fill that created the Island in 1937 is approximately 20 to 50 feet thick, depending on location on the island.[picryl/U.S. National Archives and Records Administration (styled below as picryl/NARA),1937]

Many low-lying coastal regions around the world are subject to these conditions. The pace at which a particular piece of land sinks is called its subsidence rate. In the case of Treasure Island the rate is high, but the developers are reengineering the soil. Without this newly fortified land, the subsidence rate would effectively double the increase in sea level at the northwest edge of the island.

Although the southern edges of the island, shown here, were built on the relatively stable and raised ground of the shoals, the north was placed over deep and soft Bay mud and sand. (Treasure Island Museum/NARA,1936)

And more mitigations are coming. The developers are using data-science-based models of SLR in the 21st century to remake the land as higher, denser, sustainable soil for its soon-to-be newly built world and community. Yerba Buena Island, also part of the redevelopment, was naturally rather than man-made. It is hilly, 340 ft above sea level and 20,000 years older, so far less vulnerable to SLR than its human-made neighbor.

In 1937, surface completed, the island was still called "Yerba Buena Shoals Fill." By 1939, as the Golden Gate International Exposition was poised to open, the fill was named Treasure Island, after the novel by Robert Louis Stevenson. (picryl/NARA)

The Treasure Island redevelopment is in progress and slated for completion in 2035. The geotechnical improvements begin at the seabed and continue up, to create higher, drier, more solid ground on which to make all new open space, parks, infrastructure, and buildings.

In 2016 the developers began to remake Treasure Island. Historic Building One, to be preserved, is seen behind the excavator. (Cross Media International, 2020)

The Treasure Island redevelopment is in progress and slated for completion in 2035. The geotechnical improvements begin at the seabed and continue up, to create higher, drier, more solid ground on which to make all new open space, parks, infrastructure, and buildings.

Other adaptations planned are an elevated pad in the southern corner, on top of the improved land, on which much of the infrastructure and almost all of the new commercial and residential real estate will be constructed. All new buildings will also be set back at least 300 ft from the perimeter of the island to protect against potential wave overtopping. Unusually large waves could be caused in the future by supercharged storms created by oceans warming due to climate change.

An analog architectural model of the man-made Treasure Island and the 1939 fairgrounds float in a tub. (Marcello Camarri ,1938, c. Ron Henggeler)

The first use of the man-made island was for the Golden Gate International Exposition, a worldâ€™s fair open for two years, 1939-1940. When the fair closed Treasure Island became a U.S. Naval Station, in service during World War II and other 20th Century conflicts to follow, including the Korean and Vietnam wars. Over the years many millions of sailors and administrative personnel were stationed on the island, or passed through on their way to deployment or retirement.

More on the origin story of the island (The San Francisco Chronicle, 1939)

The tale of the making of both the old and the new island is a tale of dredging. This is a 1930s suction pump used in the process. (picyrl/NARA)

Like many sites across the country and around the world, parts of the islandâ€™s soil were contaminated due to military-industrial practices from 1941 -1997, when the base was decommissioned.

In 1936 the ship "San Joaquin", a pipeline dredge, siphoned underwater shoal sediment, largely sand, to create a new place on top of Yerba Buena Shoals. (picyrl/NARA)

Since the detection of toxins, including radiation in the soil, the Navy has been working to decontaminate the island. 75% of Treasure Island has been cleaned and certified safe by the State of California for current and planned future uses. The U.S. Navyâ€™s search for, identification, and mitigation of toxins continues.

The "San Joaquin" dredge spews sand in a different locoation to build up another part of the new island--most likely the causeway to Yerba Buena Island, in the backgroun. [San Francisco History Center/San Francisco Public Library, styled below as (SFHC/SFPL)]

Some of the pipelines used to make the island were a mile long. This view of dredging is from the site that would become the Golden Gate International Exposition's Hall of Transportation, aka Building Two. (picyrl/NARA, 1937)
